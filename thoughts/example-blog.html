Cabin Fever:

Weekend Cabin Feature: Gervasutti Hut, Italy
AUTHOR: JUSTIN RANDOLPH

Sometimes you just know — love at first sight and all that — and the “Llano Exit Strategy,” is it’s lyrically called, was born in an instant. Four couples from Austin, Texas, have been friends for more than two decades. They vacation together, travel together, and have longed dreamed of building a base camp together within an hour of the city — a quick weekend escape hatch. One day they were on vacation in Llano, on the LLano River west of Austin, and they stumbled upon this little parcel, fell in love with it, and bought it a month later.

Architect Matt Garcia had worked on a house in Austin with one of the couples, so they commissioned him for help. Their goals were to have four cabins, one per couple, that provided minimal living with a larger “commons” building that had a big kitchen, dining, and living space. The cabins needed privacy, plus views of the surroundings, and they wanted them to be as energy efficient as possible and also to use the smallest footprint. The siting and vegetation were planned by one member of the group, who’s a landscape architect, with an eye toward maximizing privacy and views.

“IT'S ABOUT THE LONGING FOR A SENSE OF PLACE, FOR SHELTER SET IN TEH LANDSCAPE... FOR SOMETHING THAT SPEAKS TO REFUGE AND DISTANCE FROM THE EVERYDAY."

The cabins were placed as close to the Llano River as its floodplain would allow — about 200 feet. Each came in around 350 square feet, at a cost of about $40,000. The exterior metal siding was chosen for its low maintenance and “coolness” while the interior of bare plywood walls enhance the natural warmth of wood and stand in contrast to the metal.

Texas summers are obviously hot and winters can be cold, so each cabin was insulated with spray foam. All the windows were thermally broken and have insulated glass, and when passive tactics aren’t quite enough for comfort, the couples can temper things with miniature Energy Star heater/air conditioning units. Irrigation is via the rainwater collection tanks atop each structure.

It’s a strategy that seems to be working.
